A real case

Mata v. Avianca (2023)

In May 2023, lawyer Steven Schwartz used ChatGPT to research case law for a federal filing. The AI generated six completely fake legal cases — complete with fabricated citations, judges, and opinions. Schwartz submitted them to court.

When opposing counsel couldn't find the cases, the judge demanded answers. Schwartz was fined $5,000 and faced professional sanctions. This became the first major public reckoning with AI hallucination in a professional setting.

High stakes

The problem isn't just law. A 2024 study found that roughly 30% of AI-generated medical summaries contained at least one factual error. If a doctor uses an AI tool that recommends the wrong medication, who is liable when the patient is harmed?

With a surge in high-stakes AI use, false information can now seriously affect someone's life.

Section 2 of 6

Ethical Issues

The core issue is accountability. There is no consensus on whether responsibility falls on the user, the company, or the engineers who built the model. This ambiguity means harm can occur with no clear path to justice.

What does this harm look like?

💊
Medical harm: A patient receives incorrect medication because an AI tool misread their allergy history, leading to a severe allergic reaction.
⚖️
Professional damage: A lawyer's career is damaged because they relied on fake legal precedents generated by AI.
📉
Equity gap: A low-income person without resources to verify AI-generated advice acts on false information and faces consequences.
📄
Job loss: A paralegal loses their job after submitting a brief containing hallucinated citations they couldn't identify.
Accountability gap

When these harms occur, victims face deep uncertainty: Should they sue the AI company? The professional who used the tool? Is anyone even responsible? This lack of clarity leaves people vulnerable and allows harm to keep happening without accountability.

Section 3 of 6

Who's Involved?

AI hallucination doesn't affect everyone equally. Here's how different groups experience the problem — and what's at stake for each of them.

🧑‍💻

General Users

Everyday people using AI for health, legal, and general questions. They are the least protected by current law and often the most vulnerable to harm from false information.

⚖️

Legal Professionals

Lawyers and paralegals who risk professional sanctions, fines, and reputational damage when hallucinated content enters a filing. They face pressure to adopt AI for efficiency but bear personal liability when it fails.

Medical Professional Note
🩺

Medical Professionals

Doctors and nurses adopting AI tools face malpractice exposure when AI errors cause patient harm. They're caught between the promise of AI assistance and the reality that they remain legally responsible for all medical decisions.

Note for medical professionals

For clinicians: Under current law, even if an AI tool gives you the wrong answer, you bear the liability for the medical outcome. The AI company's terms of service typically disclaim responsibility entirely.

🏢

AI Companies

OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and others are currently shielded by liability disclaimers in their terms of service. They have the most to gain financially but the least legal exposure under current frameworks.

🏛️

Regulators & Policymakers

Bodies like the FTC, EU enforcement agencies, and Congress are struggling to keep pace with the technology — often without deep technical understanding.

Section 4 of 6

Deeper Dive

This section goes deeper on the technical and regulatory background. Feel free to skim or expand the parts that interest you.

Large Language Models like ChatGPT and Claude generate text by predicting the next most likely word based on patterns in their training data. They don't check facts or verify truth — they recognize patterns: "legal citations usually look like this" or "medical advice usually sounds like that."

This is why hallucination happens. The model confidently generates fake citations that follow the correct format because it's learned the pattern, not the facts. It's doing exactly what it was designed to do: predict plausible next words. It just can't distinguish between something "plausible sounding" and something actually true.

Hallucination isn't necessarily a bug that can be fixed — it's fundamental to how these models work. Some newer tools let AI connect to databases or search engines for verification, but most AI tools people use daily have no native fact-checking mechanism.

The EU AI Act (2024) is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation, requiring risk assessments, human oversight, and imposing penalties up to 6% of global revenue for high-risk systems — like those used in healthcare and legal practice.

The US has no equivalent federal law. It relies on voluntary commitments and sparse state regulations. This gap reveals competing values: the EU prioritizes precaution and protection, while the US favors innovation and self-regulation.

Section 5 of 6

Options for Action

Who should bear responsibility when AI causes harm? Here are three approaches — each with different trade-offs.

Users must verify all AI outputs before relying on them

Under this approach, users and professionals bear the burden of checking every AI-generated claim before acting on it — similar to how liability works today under many companies' terms of service.

Example: A lawyer relies on AI-generated citations without checking and gets sanctioned.

Pros

  • Simple — no new laws
  • Encourages critical thinking
  • Allows innovation

Cons

  • Unrealistic for non-experts
  • Unfair to vulnerable users
  • All burden on the least-equipped
Rights lens: weak protection for accurate information Justice lens: burdens those with least resources
Ethical question: Is it fair to expect expert-level verification from everyday users?

Most protective for users

AI companies are responsible for harms caused by their systems

This approach would expose providers to legal liability when their models produce harmful misinformation — creating stronger incentives for safety investment and clearer paths to compensation.

Example: A patient is harmed by incorrect AI medical advice and sues the AI company.

Pros

  • Strong safety incentives
  • Clear compensation for harm
  • Pushes companies to improve

Cons

  • Hard to prove causation
  • May slow innovation
  • Could limit AI deployment
Rights lens: prioritizes user protection Utilitarian lens: long-term safety vs. short-term speed
Ethical lens: Balances safety vs. innovation; prioritizes user protection.

Responsibility is split between companies, professionals, and users by role

Regulators would set disclosure rules, error-rate transparency, and tiered liability so accountability matches who had the power and expertise to prevent harm.

Example: Doctors verify AI suggestions while companies disclose error rates and share liability depending on context.

Pros

  • Balanced and nuanced
  • Protects non-experts
  • Shared accountability

Cons

  • Complex to implement
  • Needs new laws
  • Hard to assign blame
Justice lens: matches duty to ability Care ethics: considers relationships between parties
Ethical principle: Responsibility should match ability and role in society.
The EU AI Act (2024) is the most comprehensive attempt so far to regulate AI at scale: risk tiers, conformity checks, and post-market monitoring for high-risk systems (including medical and legal tools). It does not fully settle liability, but it sets precedent the US has not matched with equivalent federal law as of 2026.
